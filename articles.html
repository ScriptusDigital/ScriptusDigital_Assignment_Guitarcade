THE BEST SOLOS OF THE 80S

Eddie Van Halen - Jump

Released in 1984 as part of Van Halen's '1984'album, 'Jump' marked a daring evolution for both the band and the instrument. While the track is instantly recognised for its commanding Oberheim synth riff, the solo remains one of Eddie Van Halen's most melodic and technically fluent performances. Where earlier tracks like 'Eruption' showcased sheer technical ability, 'Jump' demonstrates restraint and phrasing to showcase a guitarist confident enough to serve the song while still leaving his signature all over it. Recorded at 5150 Studios using his custom "Frankenstrat" through a cracked Marshall, Eddie blends hammer-ons, dive bombs, and harmonics with precision. The solo lasts barely 20 seconds yet it distils everything that made him a revolution in modern rock: a blend of innovation, feel, and unfiltered joy. Decades later, "Jump" stands not just as a hit single, but as proof that virtuosity and pop accessibility could coexist without compromise

Yngwie Malmsteen - Far Beyond the Sun

When Swedish guitarist Yngwie Malmsteen released ‘Rising Force” in 1984, it was as if classical composition had crashed headlong into heavy metal. ‘Far Beyond the Sun’ was Yngwie’s manifesto; a declaration that guitar could be both art and athleticism. Taking inspiration from Paganini, Bach, and Baroque counterpoint, Yngwie built a new genre: neoclassical metal. His technique was relentless – rapid-fire alternate picking, arpeggios sweeping across octaves, and harmonic minor runs executed with excellent precision. Yet behind the speed lies musical intent every flurry of notes is meticulously structured, every cadence purposeful. The production, sparse and focused, places the guitar dad centre, demanding the listener’s full attention. ‘Far Beyond the Sun’ didn’t just raise the bar, it redefined it, inspiring a generation of players to approach the fretboard like concert violinists. Bonus points if you can spot the bum note at 3:35.

Slash - Sweet Child O' Mine

Appearing on ‘Appetite for Destruction’ (1987), ‘Sweet Child O’ Mine’ transformed Slash from Sunset Strip fixture to global icon. The song’s opening riff – famously written as a warmup exercise – became one of rock’s most memorable intros, but it’s the solo that seals its legacy; lyrical, melodic, emotive and impeccably phrased. Unlike the hyper-speed acrobatics dominating late-‘80s guitar culture, Slash’s playing here is almost vocal in quality. Recorded using a Gibson Les Paul copy through a cranked Marshall JCM800, the tone is warm, round and brilliantly expressive. The second solo builds to an incredible climax, a masterclass in tension and release. In a decade obsessed with flash, Slash reminded the world that feel could still outshine finesse. ‘Sweet Child O’ Mine’ isn’t just a song, it’s a study in emotional architecture through six strings.

Joe Satriani - Surfing with the Alien

Joe Satriani’s ‘Surfing with the Alien’ (1987) brought instrumental guitar into the mainstream without a singer of gimmick, just tone, melody and enthusiasm. The title track, powered by Satriani’s signature phrasing and whammy-bar dynamics, moves with vocal-like fluidity tat set him apart from the shred crowd. Every phrase feels sung rather than played; every bend, articulate and deliberate. User a Kramer Pacer and an Early Seventies Marshall Model 1959 Super Lead 100-Watt head, Satriani achieved a tone both smooth and biting, polished yet unpredictable. His legato runs and tapped harmonics are seamlessly woven into memorable melodies, a rare combination of intellect and accessibility. ‘Surfing with the Alien’ proved that virtuosity could be hummable, and in doing so, opened the door for countless instrumental guitar albums that followed.

Stevie Ray Vaughan - Texas Flood

 ‘Texas Flood’ (1983) landed like a thunderclap, serving as proof that the blues hadn’t died, it had simply been waiting for the right hands. The title track, a slow-burning 12-bar lament, captures Stevie Ray Vaughn at his most elemental: channelling the soul of Albert King and the fire of Hendrix in equal measure. Recorded live in the studio with almost no overdubs, it breathes and sweats in real time. Vauhan’s tone, carved from his battered 1962/63 Fender Stratocaster “Number one” and a Dumbleland 300 SL amplifier, borrowed from Jackson Browne shifts between silk and sandpaper. Each bend aches, each phrase testifies. While the decade chased perfection through speed and polish, Vauhan tore it down to touch and feel. ‘Texas Flood’ wasn’t retro, it was a revival, and proof that feeling, phrased right, could still level a room.
